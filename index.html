The Lazy Student's Dream: Can ChatGPT Actually Pass an Engineering Course?

Gokul Puthumanaillam, Melkior Ornik
University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign
Paper Code

Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into the capability of Large Language Models (LLMs) to successfully complete a semester-long undergraduate control systems course. Through systematic evaluation of approximately 115 course deliverables, including homework assignments, midterm examinations, programming projects, and a final exam, we assess LLM performance using ChatGPT under a ``minimal effort" protocol that simulates realistic student usage patterns. The investigation employs a rigorous testing methodology across multiple assessment formats, from auto-graded multiple choice questions to complex Python programming tasks and long-form analytical writing. Our analysis provides quantitative insights into AI's strengths and limitations in handling mathematical formulations, coding challenges, and theoretical concepts in control systems engineering. The findings inform critical discussions about course design adaptation in response to AI advancement, moving beyond simple prohibition towards thoughtful integration of these tools in engineering education. This research represents a whole-course case study examining LLM performance in mathematics-based engineering education, offering insights for educators and institutions navigating the intersection of artificial intelligence and technical education.
